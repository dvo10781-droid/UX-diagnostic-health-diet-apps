Origin of This Work
"I wasn't lazy. The apps just weren't built
for someone like me."
I've spent years trying to lose weight. I tried app after app — the ones you see advertised, the ones with the best ratings. I'd start with real motivation. Then, slowly, I'd stop. Not because I gave up, but because every single one kept asking me to live a life I don't live.

Track your Greek yogurt. Here's a 45-minute recipe for one. Use a meal plan that costs more than my family grocery budget.
I'm a Vietnamese woman in my forties. I work from home. I cook warm food — rice, soups, shared dishes — for my whole family on a tight budget. I enjoy all kinds of food. But eating a cold salad as a main meal? It is cold. It is dry. It does not fill a family.

At some point I stopped blaming myself and started asking: what if the apps are the problem? That question became this diagnostic — using the same bias framework I learned from Google's Responsible AI for Developers course.

Bias Types Identified — Google Responsible AI for Developers Framework · v2 adds Implicit Bias
Selection Bias
These apps were optimized for users who succeeded with Western eating patterns, conventional schedules, and standard household structures. The non-default user is invisible in the training data.
Reporting Bias
The apps surface and reward behaviors that fit their model: daily streaks, precise individual logging, standard sleep patterns. Night-shift schedules and shared dishes appear as "no data" or failures.
Group Attribution Bias
Even when cultural preference data is collected (Vietnamese, Thai, Japanese cuisines), the same Western defaults are delivered. Collecting data is not the same as using it.
Implicit Bias — v2
The system encodes assumptions about who the user is through default choices, visual language, and what needs no explanation. No rule says "Western users only." The bias lives in what the system treats as normal.

Failure 06 · Implicit Bias · v2 Addition
The Unencumbered Individual Assumption
Implicit BiasSelection Bias
Critical
System Assumption
The user is an individual managing their own body, time, and food choices
Self-care is separable from care for others
"Food freedom" and "no restrictive diets" are universally available states
Behavioral change is primarily a personal psychology problem
"Love yourself first" is actionable advice for all users equally
My Lived Experience
I am not one person making choices for myself. I am a system — a logistics operation running food, schedule, budget, and culture for my whole family.
My food choices are constrained by what my children will eat, what my budget allows this week, what takes 30 minutes after a night shift, and what my culture recognizes as a real meal that nourishes people.
"Love yourself first" is right advice. But it assumes the self is available to be loved first. For a mother running a household system, the self comes last — not from low self-worth, but because the system she runs has no pause button.
Every review says Noom was "built by psychologists." That psychology was built for a self that is separable from its context. Mine is not.
Diagnostic Note
Root Cause — Implicit Bias
The behavioral psychology research underlying these apps was conducted on a population where the individual self is assumed to be the primary unit of change. No rule says "not for mothers." The bias lives in what was never questioned: that the user has a self separable from the people she feeds, the budget she manages, and the schedule she does not control.

This is implicit bias — not in an explicit decision, but in structural invisibility. The caregiver as primary subject was never in the room when the model was built.
→ Clinical AI Connection Mental health AI tools trained on individualist behavioral markers misread caregiver lives as pathology. Waking at 2am = depression signal in training data. For a Vietnamese mother on night shift = Tuesday. Eating irregularly = disordered eating signal. For a mother who eats after her children are fed = love. The system reads her life as disorder because it was built to read a different life as normal.

05 — Key Insights
What Dropping Out of These Apps Actually Means
🔗
Dropout is a design signal, not a motivation signal
Selection bias means users who persist are already those the system was built for. My dropout was the system working as designed — just not for me.
🏠
Eating is a household system, not an individual task
No app I tested was designed with a household as the primary unit. One cook, one pot, family eats — this is how most of the world actually operates.
🌾
Real home-cooked food is being penalized as "yellow"
The food pyramid places whole grains at its base. Noom places rice in the yellow caution category. The food I cook from scratch is classified as something to limit.
💸
Stacked payments are a trust collapse, not just friction
I track BetterMe's cancellation date on Google Calendar. That cognitive and financial burden is a system design choice — and it disproportionately affects budget-constrained users.
🧬
Collecting preference data ≠ using it
Noom asked my cuisines. I said Vietnamese, Thai, Japanese. It served me couscous. This gap is Group Attribution Bias — and it appears identically in clinical AI when demographic data is collected but outputs don't change.
🌙
Non-standard schedules are invisible to these systems
The sleep tracker shows 0% on my night-shift day. Reporting Bias means the system reads absence of standard-pattern data as failure rather than as a valid user life.
06 — Design Principles
What Would Have Made These Apps Work for Me
P1
Design for meals, not individuals
Support household-scale meals as first-class. One cook, one pot, family eats — this is how most of the world eats.
P2
If you collect it, use it
Asking about cuisine preferences and then ignoring them is worse than not asking. It signals the system is performing personalization without delivering it.
P3
Classify food in cultural context
Rice is not a caution food for Vietnamese households — it is the foundation of a nutritionally-sound diet. Classification systems must reflect the population being served.
P4
Favor approximation for shared meals
For soups and shared dishes, a rough estimate mode beats a precise logger that nobody uses past week two.
P5
Model non-standard schedules as valid
Night shift work and fragmented sleep are real patterns, not compliance failures. Design for them.
P6
Transparent, non-stacked pricing
For budget-constrained households, stacked in-app purchases are a betrayal of trust. Total cost must be honest upfront.

08 — Reflection
What I Actually Learned from Failing at Every Diet App
"The apps weren't broken. They just weren't built with me in mind — and that's a harder problem to name when you're the one feeling like the failure."

Writing this took longer than I expected — not because the analysis was hard, but because I had to stop blaming myself first. Every time I dropped out of a diet app, I assumed it was a discipline problem. Now I have a name for what it actually was: selection bias, reporting bias, group attribution bias. The Google Responsible AI for Developers course gave me the vocabulary. My own experience provided the evidence.

The food I cook at home — cơm, canh, kho, cháo — is real food, made from scratch, nutritionally grounded. The food pyramid agrees. Noom classified it as a yellow caution food. That's not a minor UX problem. That's a core assumption encoded as science.

What surprised me is how naturally the diagnostic framework came. I've spent years as a medical interpreter — finding exactly where communication breaks down between two parties who don't share the same context. Where does the system's model of the user diverge from the actual user? That question has the same answer in a diet app and in a clinical AI tool. The stakes are just different.

The deepest finding came last — and it came from living, not from analysis. These apps keep saying "find time for yourself," "love yourself first," "you deserve this." That advice is not wrong. But it assumes the self is separable from its context. For a mother, it is not. My food is my family's food. My time is the family's time. My relationship with food is actually a relationship between me, my children, my budget, my culture, and the clock. I am not one person making choices for myself. I am a system — a logistics operation. My meal decisions are not personal preferences. They are the household running. The apps were not built for that subject. And that absence — that structural invisibility — is implicit bias. Not a rule. Not a decision. Just: she was never in the room when the model was built.

This is v2. Failure 06 is new. The framework keeps growing — because the experience keeps teaching.
